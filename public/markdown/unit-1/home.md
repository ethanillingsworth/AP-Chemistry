# Unit 1 | Atomic Structure

**Exam Weight:** 7–9% of the AP Exam

Unit 1 explores the atomic theory of matter, the foundational premise of chemistry. It builds the bridge between the mass of a sample and the number of atoms it contains, while explaining how the arrangement of electrons dictates the behavior of the elements.

## [1.1 Moles and Molar Mass](/unit-1/topic-1.1)

- **The Mole Concept:** Connects the macroscopic mass of a substance to the actual number of particles undergoing chemical changes.
- **Avogadro’s Number:** $6.022 \times 10^{23}$ particles/mole provides the conversion factor between moles and the number of constituent particles.
- **Molar Mass:** The average mass of one particle (in amu) is numerically equal to the molar mass of that substance in grams.
- **Calculations:** Using dimensional analysis and the mole concept to calculate quantities of a substance or its relative number of particles.

## [1.2 Mass Spectroscopy](/unit-1/topic-1.2)

- **Isotopes:** Mass spectrometry identifies the isotopes of an element and their relative abundance in nature.
- **Average Atomic Mass:** Calculated as a weighted average of the isotopic masses of all naturally occurring isotopes of an element.
- **Interpreting Spectra:** The mass spectrum of a single element is used to explain the quantitative relationship between the mass spectrum and the masses of the element's isotopes.

## [1.3 Elemental Composition of Pure Substances](/unit-1/topic-1.3)

- **Pure Substances:** Composed of atoms, ions, or molecules in fixed proportions as described by a formula unit.
- **Law of Definite Proportions:** The ratio of the masses of constituent elements in any pure sample of a compound is always the same.
- **Empirical Formula:** Represents the lowest whole-number ratio of atoms of the elements in a compound.

## [1.4 Composition of Mixtures](/unit-1/topic-1.4)

- **Mixtures vs. Pure Substances:** While pure substances contain a single type of molecule or formula unit, mixtures contain molecules or formula units of two or more types in varying proportions.
- **Purity Analysis:** Elemental analysis is used to determine the relative number of atoms in a substance and to verify its purity.
- **Quantitative Analysis:** Relating elemental composition by mass to the overall composition of substances within a mixture.

## [1.5 Atomic Structure & Electron Configuration](/unit-1/topic-1.5)

- **Coulomb’s Law:** Used to predict the ionization energy of an electron based on its distance from the nucleus and the nuclear charge.
- **Shell Model:** Electrons are organized into shells, subshells, and orbitals based on the Aufbau principle.
- **Valence Electrons:** The outermost electrons of an atom that determine its chemical reactivity and bonding behavior.

## [1.6 Photoelectron Spectroscopy (PES)](/unit-1/topic-1.6)

- **Experimental Evidence:** PES measures the energies of electrons in specific shells to provide evidence for the shell/subshell model.
- **Peak Position:** The position of each peak relates to the binding energy (energy required to remove an electron from that subshell).
- **Peak Height:** Proportional to the number of electrons residing in that specific subshell.

## [1.7 Periodic Trends](/unit-1/topic-1.7)

- **Periodicity:** The organization of the periodic table reflects predictable patterns in element properties as a function of atomic number.
- **Reactivity:** Periodic trends are used to explain the relationship between an element's position on the table and its chemical reactivity.

## [1.8 Valence Electrons and Ionic Compounds](/unit-1/topic-1.8)

- **Bonding Likelihood:** The interaction between valence electrons and nuclei determines the likelihood of two elements forming a chemical bond.
- **Ionic Charges:** Common charges for ions are governed by an element's location on the periodic table and its number of valence electrons.
- **Analogous Compounds:** Elements in the same column (group) tend to form similar compounds because they have the same number of valence electrons.
