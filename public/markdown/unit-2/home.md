# Unit 2 | Molecular and Ionic Compound Structure & Properties

**Exam Weight:** 7–9% of the AP Exam

Unit 2 focuses on how atomic structure relates to the macroscopic properties of substances. It explores the structure and arrangement of atoms, ions, or molecules, and how the chemical bonds between them determine both the chemical and physical properties of materials.

## [2.1 Types of Bonds](/unit-2/topic-2.1)

- **Electronegativity Trends:** Electronegativity increases across a period from left to right and decreases down a group.
- **Nonpolar Covalent Bonds:** Formed when valence electrons are shared between atoms of similar electronegativity.
- **Polar Covalent Bonds:** Formed when valence electrons are shared between atoms of unequal electronegativity, creating a partial negative charge on the more electronegative atom.
- **Ionic vs. Covalent Classification:** Generally, metal and non-metal bonds are ionic, while bonds between two nonmetals are covalent, but examining compound properties is the most accurate way to characterize the bond.

## [2.2 Intramolecular Force and Potential Energy](/unit-2/topic-2.2)

- **Potential Energy Graphs:** A graph of potential energy versus distance illustrates the equilibrium bond length (lowest potential energy) and bond energy (energy required to separate atoms).
- **Bond Length and Energy:** Bond length depends on atom size and bond order; higher-order bonds (e.g., double or triple) are shorter and have larger bond energies.
- **Coulomb's Law in Bonds:** The interaction strength between ions is stronger with larger ionic charges and smaller ionic radii.

## [2.3 Structure of Ionic Solids](/unit-2/topic-2.3)

- **Crystal Lattice:** Cations and anions are arranged in a systematic, periodic 3-D array.
- **Maximizing Stability:** This 3-D arrangement maximizes the attractive forces between cations and anions while minimizing repulsive forces.

## [2.4 Structure of Metals and Alloys](/unit-2/topic-2.4)

- **Metallic Bonding:** Represented as an array of positive metal ions surrounded by delocalized valence electrons, often called a "sea of electrons".
- **Interstitial Alloys:** Formed when smaller atoms fill the interstitial spaces between larger atoms in the metallic lattice.
- **Substitutional Alloys:** Formed when atoms of comparable radii substitute for one another within the crystal lattice.

## [2.5 Lewis Diagrams](/unit-2/topic-2.5)

- **Diagram Principles:** Lewis diagrams represent molecules and are constructed using established principles for arranging valence electrons.
- **Model Limitations:** There are limitations to Lewis structures, particularly when dealing with molecules that possess an odd number of valence electrons.

## [2.6 Resonance and Formal Charge](/unit-2/topic-2.6)

- **Resonance:** Included as a refinement when more than one equivalent Lewis structure can be drawn, providing more accurate predictions of molecular properties.
- **Formal Charge:** Used alongside the octet rule as a criterion to determine the best model among several possible, valid, non-equivalent Lewis diagrams.

## [2.7 VSEPR and Bond Hybridization](/unit-2/topic-2.7)

- **VSEPR Theory:** Uses the Coulombic repulsion between electrons to predict the geometric arrangement of electron pairs around a central atom.
- **Predicting Properties:** Both Lewis diagrams and VSEPR theory are used to predict molecular geometry, bond angles, relative bond energies, bond lengths, dipole moments, and hybridization.
- **Hybridization:** Describes the arrangement of electrons around a central atom, with $sp$ having $180^\circ$ angles, $sp^2$ having $120^\circ$ angles, and $sp^3$ having $109.5^\circ$ angles.
- **Sigma and Pi Bonds:** Bond formation involves atomic orbital overlap; sigma bonds are stronger and have greater bond energy than pi bonds, while pi bonds prevent bond rotation.
