# Unit 4 | Chemical Reactions

**Exam Weight:** 7–9% of the AP Exam

Unit 4 introduces the language of chemistry through chemical equations, exploring how atoms rearrange to form new substances. It covers physical and chemical changes, stoichiometry, and different types of chemical reactions, including precipitation, acid-base, and oxidation-reduction.

## [4.1 Introduction for Reactions](/unit-4/topic-4.1)

- **Chemical Changes:** Processes where one or more substances are transformed into new substances with different properties.
- **Evidence of Reaction:** Observable signs such as temperature change, color change, gas evolution, or precipitate formation indicate a chemical reaction.

## [4.2 Net Ionic Equations](/unit-4/topic-4.2)

- **Balanced Equations:** Chemical equations must be balanced to satisfy the law of conservation of mass, ensuring the same number of each type of atom on both sides.
- **Net Ionic Equations:** Show only the chemical species that actively participate in the reaction, omitting spectator ions that remain unchanged in the solution.

## [4.3 Representations of Reactions](/unit-4/topic-4.3)

- **Particulate Models:** Visualizing chemical reactions at the particulate level helps illustrate the conservation of atoms and the rearrangement of bonds.
- **Proportionality:** Particulate drawings must accurately reflect the stoichiometric ratios of reactants and products present in the balanced equation.

## [4.4 Physical and Chemical Changes](/unit-4/topic-4.4)

- **Physical Changes:** Involve changes in intermolecular forces, such as phase transitions or dissolving, without altering the actual chemical bonds within the molecules.
- **Chemical Changes:** Involve the breaking and forming of intramolecular chemical bonds to create entirely new substances.

## [4.5 Stoichiometry](/unit-4/topic-4.5)

- **Mole Ratios:** Using the stoichiometric coefficients from a balanced chemical equation to convert between moles of different substances in a reaction.
- **Limiting Reactants:** The reactant that is completely consumed first in a chemical reaction, which determines the maximum amount of product that can be formed.
- **Percent Yield:** Comparing the actual experimental yield to the theoretical calculated yield to determine the efficiency of a reaction.

## [4.6 Introduction to Titration](/unit-4/topic-4.6)

- **Titration Concept:** An experimental analytical technique used to determine the concentration of an unknown solution (analyte) by reacting it with a solution of known concentration (titrant).
- **Equivalence Point:** The precise point in a titration when the moles of titrant added are stoichiometrically equivalent to the moles of analyte initially present.

## [4.7 Types of Chemical Reactions](/unit-4/topic-4.7)

- **Precipitation Reactions:** Occur when two aqueous solutions mix to form an insoluble solid product, known as a precipitate.
- **Acid-Base Reactions:** Involve the transfer of a proton ($H^+$) from an acid to a base.
- **Redox Reactions:** Involve the transfer of electrons between reactants, resulting in changes in oxidation states.

## [4.8 Introduction to Acid-Base Reactions](/unit-4/topic-4.8)

- **Brønsted-Lowry Model:** Defines acids as proton donors and bases as proton acceptors.
- **Conjugate Pairs:** Acid-base reactions involve conjugate acid-base pairs that differ by a single proton ($H^+$).

## [4.9 Oxidation-Reduction (Redox) Reactions](/unit-4/topic-4.9)

- **Oxidation States:** A bookkeeping system to track electron transfer; oxidation involves losing electrons (increase in oxidation number), while reduction involves gaining electrons (decrease in oxidation number).
- **Half-Reactions:** Redox reactions can be separated into oxidation and reduction half-reactions to help identify the transfer of electrons and to balance complex equations.
