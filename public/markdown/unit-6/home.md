# Unit 6 | Thermochemistry

**Exam Weight:** 7–9% of the AP Exam

Unit 6 introduces the role of energy in chemical and physical processes. It covers heat transfer, calorimetry, phase changes, and the various methods for calculating the enthalpy changes of reactions, providing the foundation for understanding thermodynamic favorability.

## [6.1 Endothermic and Exothermic Processes](/unit-6/topic-6.1)

- **System and Surroundings:** The system is the specific part of the universe being studied, while the surroundings are everything else.
- **Heat Transfer:** Temperature changes are driven by the transfer of heat ($q$) between the system and its surroundings.
- **Exothermic Processes:** Release heat from the system to the surroundings ($\Delta H < 0$), causing the surroundings' temperature to increase.
- **Endothermic Processes:** Absorb heat from the surroundings into the system ($\Delta H > 0$), causing the surroundings' temperature to decrease.

## [6.2 Energy Diagrams](/unit-6/topic-6.2)

- **Reaction Energy Profiles:** Visual representations showing the potential energy of the system as a reaction proceeds from reactants to products.
- **Exothermic Profiles:** Products have lower potential energy than reactants.
- **Endothermic Profiles:** Products have higher potential energy than reactants.

## [6.3 Heat Transfer and Thermal Equilibrium](/unit-6/topic-6.3)

- **Kinetic Energy Transfer:** Particles of a warmer body have a higher average kinetic energy than those of a cooler body. Collisions transfer energy from the hotter to the cooler body.
- **Thermal Equilibrium:** Heat transfer continues until both systems reach the same temperature and their particles have the same average kinetic energy.

## [6.4 Heat Capacity and Calorimetry](/unit-6/topic-6.4)

- **Specific Heat Capacity ($c$):** The amount of energy required to raise the temperature of 1 gram of a substance by 1°C (or 1 K).
- **Calorimetry Equation:** Heat transfer is calculated using $q = mc\Delta T$, where $m$ is mass and $\Delta T$ is the change in temperature.
- **Conservation of Energy:** In an isolated system, the heat lost by one substance equals the heat gained by another ($q_{lost} = -q_{gained}$).

## [6.5 Energy of Phase Changes](/unit-6/topic-6.5)

- **Phase Transitions:** Energy is required to overcome intermolecular forces during melting and vaporization (endothermic), while energy is released during freezing and condensation (exothermic).
- **Constant Temperature:** During a phase change, the temperature remains constant because the added or removed energy changes the potential energy (intermolecular forces) rather than the kinetic energy.
- **Enthalpy of Phase Changes:** Heat required for phase changes is calculated using $q = n\Delta H_{fus}$ (melting) or $q = n\Delta H_{vap}$ (vaporizing).

## [6.6 Introduction to Enthalpy of Reaction](/unit-6/topic-6.6)

- **Enthalpy ($\Delta H$):** The heat absorbed or released by a chemical reaction at constant pressure.
- **Thermochemical Equations:** Balanced equations that include the enthalpy change, allowing for stoichiometric calculations relating the moles of reactants/products to the amount of heat transferred.

## [6.7 Bond Enthalpies](/unit-6/topic-6.7)

- **Breaking vs. Forming Bonds:** Energy is always required to break bonds (endothermic), and energy is always released when bonds form (exothermic).
- **Calculating $\Delta H_{rxn}$:** The overall enthalpy of a reaction can be estimated by comparing the energy required to break the reactant bonds to the energy released by forming the product bonds: $\Delta H_{rxn} = \Sigma(\text{Bond Energies Broken}) - \Sigma(\text{Bond Energies Formed})$.

## [6.8 Enthalpy of Formation](/unit-6/topic-6.8)

- **Standard Enthalpy of Formation ($\Delta H_f^\circ$):** The enthalpy change when 1 mole of a compound is formed from its elements in their standard states.
- **Calculating $\Delta H_{rxn}^\circ$:** The standard enthalpy of a reaction can be calculated using the enthalpies of formation of the products and reactants: $\Delta H_{rxn}^\circ = \Sigma \Delta H_f^\circ(\text{Products}) - \Sigma \Delta H_f^\circ(\text{Reactants})$.

## [6.9 Hess's Law](/unit-6/topic-6.9)

- **State Function:** Enthalpy is a state function, meaning it depends only on the initial and final states, not the pathway taken.
- **Additive Reactions:** If a reaction is carried out in a series of steps, $\Delta H$ for the overall reaction equals the sum of the enthalpy changes for the individual steps.
- **Manipulating Equations:** If a reaction is reversed, the sign of $\Delta H$ is flipped. If the coefficients of a reaction are multiplied by an integer, the $\Delta H$ is multiplied by that same integer.
