# Unit 7 | Equilibrium

**Exam Weight:** 7–9% of the AP Exam

Unit 7 explores the dynamic nature of chemical equilibrium. It covers reversible reactions, the mathematical relationship between reactant and product concentrations at equilibrium, and how chemical systems respond to external stresses according to Le Châtelier's Principle.

## [7.1 Introduction to Equilibrium](/unit-7/topic-7.1)

- **Dynamic Equilibrium:** A state in a reversible reaction where the forward and reverse reaction rates are equal, resulting in no net change in macroscopic properties.
- **Reversibility:** Many chemical and physical processes can occur in both the forward and reverse directions.

## [7.2 Direction of Reversible Reactions](/unit-7/topic-7.2)

- **Approaching Equilibrium:** If a system is not at equilibrium, the rates of the forward and reverse reactions are unequal, causing the system to shift until equilibrium is reached.

## [7.3 Reaction Quotient and Equilibrium Constant](/unit-7/topic-7.3)

- **Reaction Quotient ($Q$):** A ratio of product concentrations (or pressures) to reactant concentrations at any given moment, each raised to the power of their stoichiometric coefficients.
- **Equilibrium Constant ($K$):** The specific value of the reaction quotient $Q$ when the system is at equilibrium. Pure solids and pure liquids are always excluded from both $Q$ and $K$ expressions.

## [7.4 Calculating the Equilibrium Constant](/unit-7/topic-7.4)

- **ICE Tables:** Initial, Change, and Equilibrium (ICE) tables are used to organize data and calculate the value of $K$ from experimental equilibrium concentrations or partial pressures.

## [7.5 Magnitude of the Equilibrium Constant](/unit-7/topic-7.5)

- **Product-Favored:** A large $K$ value ($K > 1$) indicates that the equilibrium mixture contains mostly products.
- **Reactant-Favored:** A small $K$ value ($K < 1$) indicates that the equilibrium mixture contains mostly reactants.

## [7.6 Properties of the Equilibrium Constant](/unit-7/topic-7.6)

- **Reversing Reactions:** When a reaction is reversed, the new equilibrium constant is the reciprocal of the original ($K_{new} = 1/K$).
- **Multiplying Coefficients:** If the coefficients of a reaction are multiplied by a factor $n$, the equilibrium constant is raised to that power ($K_{new} = K^n$).
- **Adding Reactions:** When adding two or more reactions, their individual $K$ values are multiplied to find the overall equilibrium constant ($K_{overall} = K_1 \times K_2$).

## [7.7 Calculating Equilibrium Concentrations](/unit-7/topic-7.7)

- **Finding Unknowns:** If the equilibrium constant ($K$) and initial concentrations are known, algebraic methods (often involving ICE tables) can be used to solve for final equilibrium concentrations or pressures.

## [7.8 Representations of Equilibrium](/unit-7/topic-7.8)

- **Particulate Models:** Interpreting and drawing particulate-level diagrams to represent the relative amounts of reactants and products present in a system at equilibrium.

## [7.9 Introduction to Le Châtelier's Principle](/unit-7/topic-7.9)

- **System Stress:** When a stress (such as a change in concentration, pressure, volume, or temperature) is applied to a system at equilibrium, the system will shift in a direction that minimizes that stress.
- **Temperature Changes:** Changing the temperature is the only stress that will permanently alter the actual value of the equilibrium constant ($K$).

## [7.10 Reaction Quotient and Le Châtelier's Principle](/unit-7/topic-7.10)

- **Predicting Shifts with $Q$:** By comparing $Q$ to $K$, you can mathematically predict the direction of a shift. If $Q < K$, the reaction shifts right (toward products). If $Q > K$, the reaction shifts left (toward reactants).

## [7.11 Introduction to Solubility Equilibria](/unit-7/topic-7.11)

- **Solubility Product Constant ($K_{sp}$):** The equilibrium constant representing the dissolution of a slightly soluble ionic compound in water.
- **Molar Solubility:** The maximum number of moles of a solute that can dissolve in a liter of solution to form a saturated solution.

## [7.12 Common-Ion Effect](/unit-7/topic-7.12)

- **Decreased Solubility:** The solubility of a salt is significantly reduced when it is dissolved in a solution that already contains one of its constituent ions (a "common ion"), as predicted by Le Châtelier's principle.

## [7.13 pH and Solubility](/unit-7/topic-7.13)

- **Acid-Base Interactions:** The solubility of an ionic compound containing a basic anion increases in acidic solutions because the added $H^+$ ions react with the basic anion, pulling the equilibrium toward further dissolution.

## [7.14 Free Energy of Dissolution](/unit-7/topic-7.14)

- **Thermodynamic Connection:** The dissolution of a salt involves both enthalpy ($\Delta H^\circ$) and entropy ($\Delta S^\circ$) changes. The thermodynamic favorability of the dissolution process is linked to the equilibrium constant.
