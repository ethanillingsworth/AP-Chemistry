# Unit 8 | Acids & Bases

**Exam Weight:** 11–15% of the AP Exam

Unit 8 applies the principles of chemical equilibrium to the behavior of acids and bases in aqueous solutions. It explores the definition of pH, the behavior of weak versus strong acids and bases, titrations, and the essential biological and chemical role of buffers.

## [8.1 Introduction to Acids and Bases](/unit-8/topic-8.1)

- **Brønsted-Lowry Model:** Acids are defined as proton ($H^+$) donors, while bases are defined as proton ($H^+$) acceptors.
- **Conjugate Pairs:** Chemical species that differ by exactly one proton (e.g., $NH_4^+$ and $NH_3$).
- **Autoionization of Water:** Water can act as both an acid and a base, autoionizing to form $H_3O^+$ and $OH^-$ with an equilibrium constant $K_w = 1.0 \times 10^{-14}$ at $25^\circ\text{C}$.

## [8.2 pH and pOH of Strong Acids and Bases](/unit-8/topic-8.2)

- **Strong Electrolytes:** Strong acids and strong bases completely dissociate in aqueous solutions.
- **Calculations:** pH and pOH are logarithmic scales used to express the concentration of hydronium and hydroxide ions: $pH = -\log[H_3O^+]$ and $pOH = -\log[OH^-]$.
- **Relationship:** In water at $25^\circ\text{C}$, the sum of pH and pOH is always 14.

## [8.3 Weak Acid and Base Equilibria](/unit-8/topic-8.3)

- **Partial Dissociation:** Weak acids and bases only partially dissociate in water, establishing a dynamic equilibrium.
- **Equilibrium Constants:** The dissociation is quantified by the acid-dissociation constant ($K_a$) or base-dissociation constant ($K_b$).
- **ICE Tables:** Used to calculate the equilibrium concentrations of ions and the resulting pH of weak acid or weak base solutions.

## [8.4 Acid-Base Reactions and Buffers](/unit-8/topic-8.4)

- **Neutralization:** Reactions between acids and bases generally have a very large equilibrium constant ($K > 1$) and proceed essentially to completion.
- **Stoichiometry:** Limiting reactant principles are used to determine the moles of excess acid or base remaining after the reaction.

## [8.5 Acid-Base Titrations](/unit-8/topic-8.5)

- **Titration Curves:** Graphical representations of pH versus the volume of titrant added.
- **Equivalence Point:** The precise point where the moles of titrant added stoichiometrically equals the moles of analyte originally present.
- **Half-Equivalence Point:** The point in a weak acid/strong base titration where exactly half of the weak acid has been neutralized, resulting in $[HA] = [A^-]$ and $pH = pK_a$.

## [8.6 Molecular Structure of Acids and Bases](/unit-8/topic-8.6)

- **Binary Acids:** Acid strength increases down a group (due to decreasing bond strength) and across a period (due to increasing electronegativity).
- **Oxyacids:** Acid strength increases with the electronegativity of the central atom and the number of terminal, highly electronegative oxygen atoms drawing electron density away from the $O-H$ bond.

## [8.7 pH and pKa](/unit-8/topic-8.7)

- **Predicting Dominant Species:** The relationship between the pH of a solution and the $pK_a$ of a weak acid determines which form of the acid is present in a higher concentration.
- **Protonation State:** If $pH < pK_a$, the fully protonated acidic form ($HA$) dominates. If $pH > pK_a$, the deprotonated conjugate base form ($A^-$) dominates.

## [8.8 Properties of Buffers](/unit-8/topic-8.8)

- **Buffer Composition:** A solution containing roughly equal concentrations of a weak acid and its conjugate base (or a weak base and its conjugate acid).
- **Resisting Change:** Buffers maintain a relatively stable pH when small amounts of strong acid or strong base are added. The weak acid neutralizes added base, and the conjugate base neutralizes added acid.

## [8.9 Henderson-Hasselbalch Equation](/unit-8/topic-8.9)

- **The Equation:** $pH = pK_a + \log([A^-]/[HA])$ allows for the direct calculation of a buffer solution's pH.
- **Buffer Design:** To create a buffer at a specific target pH, chemists select a weak acid with a $pK_a$ as close to the target pH as possible.

## [8.10 Buffer Capacity](/unit-8/topic-8.10)

- **Defining Capacity:** The amount of strong acid or strong base a buffer can neutralize before its pH begins to change significantly.
- **Increasing Capacity:** Buffer capacity is directly related to the absolute concentrations of the buffer components; higher concentrations of weak acid and conjugate base yield a higher buffer capacity.
